What this is

A model that shows its work.

It projects a strikeout total for every MLB starting pitcher, compares that projection to sportsbook over/under lines, and ranks whatever positive-expected-value edges turn up. Then it gets out of the way — no manual overrides, no quiet deletions.

01 / RUNS ITSELF

Refreshes on its own

Lineups, weather, and odds pull in several times a day as they update — no one's thumb on the scale between data and projection.

02 / LOGGED EARLY

Timestamped before first pitch

Every number is written down before the game starts. There's no version of a projection made after the fact.

03 / RECONCILED NIGHTLY

Graded while you sleep

Actual strikeouts get matched to the logged projection overnight, automatically, and posted whether the model was right or wrong.

The model

From box score to probability.

A LightGBM model with a Poisson objective handles the point estimate. Five quantile models trace the shape of the distribution around it. A monotone curve turns that shape into a probability of clearing any given line.

STEP 01

Feature build

Pitcher form, opponent lineup, workload, and context assembled per start.

STEP 02

Point estimate

LightGBM, Poisson objective — a single expected strikeout count.

STEP 03

Distribution

p10 / p25 / p50 / p75 / p90 quantile models trace the realistic range.

STEP 04

PCHIP curve

A smooth monotone curve through the quantiles prices any line's over/under odds.

Why Poisson

Strikeouts are a count, not a continuous number, and Poisson-style objectives are built for exactly that: a non-negative outcome with a long right tail on nights a starter runs deep and racks up whiffs.

Why five separate models

A single point estimate can't tell you how confident to be. The quantile spread does — a tight p10–p90 range means a cleaner bet against the line; a wide one means the model is hedging, and the odds should reflect that.

Retraining

Weekly, on an expanding window of every start logged since 2022 — new results fold in without discarding the seasons behind them.

Early-season prior

A Marcel-style regressed prior from the prior season anchors projections in April, fading out as batters-faced accumulates — roughly half-weighted by the time a starter has logged 70 batters faced this season.

Features

The lineup card.

Ranked by importance to the model, not by how easy each one is to explain.

01

Swing-and-miss form

Leadoff — heaviest weight

CSW%, SwStr%, and K% as exponentially-weighted averages with a ~5-start half-life. CSW% stabilizes faster than K%, which is why it leads the lineup rather than K% itself.

02

Opponent lineup K% vs. hand

Matchup

Slot-weighted strikeout rate of the nine projected hitters against the pitcher's throwing hand — blended 60% last 30 days, 40% full season.

03

Expected workload

Volume

Recent batters-faced average combined with the game's run environment — Vegas total when it's posted, otherwise both teams' recent scoring.

04

Stabilizers and context

Situational

K-BB%, fastball velocity trend as a fatigue flag, park strikeout factor, home-plate umpire tendency, temperature and wind at outdoor parks, home/away, days of rest.

05

Early-season prior

Regression anchor

A Marcel-style prior from last season, blended in by sample size — the model leans on it in April, on current form by June.

—

Batter-vs-pitcher history

Deliberately excluded

Mostly noise below roughly 25 plate appearances of head-to-head history — left out on purpose rather than down-weighted.
